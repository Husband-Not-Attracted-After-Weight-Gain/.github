# My Husband Isn’t Attracted To Me Because I Gained Weight (My Husband Says My Weight Is A Problem)

Weight gain is one of those deeply personal things that can quietly change the way you feel about yourself. But when you start to sense that it has also changed the way your husband looks at you, that hurt cuts much deeper. If your husband has said something about your weight, or if you just feel his distance and suspect why, know this: you are not alone, and your pain is valid. What you do with this moment matters more than the number on any scale.

I often hear from women who are navigating this exact situation, caught between feeling rejected by the person who promised to love them and feeling ashamed of a body that has simply changed with time, stress, health challenges, or the demands of life. The goal here is not to tell you whose fault it is. The goal is to help you understand what is really happening and what you can do about it, with clarity and without losing yourself in the process.

**Understanding What Your Husband Might Actually Mean**

When a husband says your weight is a problem, those words can land like a slap. But before you spiral into self-blame or rage, it helps to think carefully about what he might actually be communicating. Sometimes it is about physical attraction, yes. But often, it is also about fear. Fear that you are not taking care of yourself. Fear that your health is at risk. Fear that the version of you he fell in love with is slipping away.

That does not make his delivery acceptable. How someone says something matters as much as what they say. But separating the message from the pain of the delivery allows you to respond with more power. Ask yourself: Is he coming from a place of cruelty, or is he coming from a place of fear and poor communication? The answer shapes everything about how you respond.

It is also worth asking yourself honestly: has your weight gain been accompanied by other changes? Less energy, pulling back from intimacy, low mood, less engagement with life together? Sometimes weight is the visible part of a bigger shift, and your husband may be reacting to all of it while only naming one piece of it.

**Your Worth Is Not Up for Debate**

Before anything else, this needs to be said clearly. Your value as a woman and as a wife is not tied to your dress size. That is not a motivational phrase. It is a fact. Weight fluctuates across a lifetime for countless reasons, many of which have nothing to do with discipline or effort. Illness, hormonal changes, stress, grief, medication, pregnancy, and aging are all real. If your husband has made you feel like you are worth less because your body has changed, that is a problem with how he is expressing himself, not a verdict on who you are.

You are allowed to feel hurt. You are allowed to feel angry. And you are also allowed to decide that you will not carry shame that was never yours to hold. Protecting your sense of self-worth in this season is not selfish. It is necessary. Because decisions made from shame almost never lead anywhere healthy, whether those decisions are about your body, your marriage, or your future.

**The Conversation You Actually Need To Have**

Avoiding a direct conversation about this will not make it go away. It will only allow resentment to grow on both sides. So the question is not whether to talk about it, but how.

Choose a time when neither of you is tired, rushed, or already irritated. Then be honest, but also curious. Let him know how his words or behavior have affected you. Not in a way designed to make him feel guilty, but in a way that opens the door to real honesty. Tell him what you felt when he said what he said. Then give him room to explain himself more fully.

Ask him directly: What are you most concerned about? Is this about attraction, or is something else going on for you? Is there something you are not saying? Sometimes that question alone unlocks a conversation that changes everything. And sometimes it confirms that his attitude is the real issue.

You also get to ask for what you need. If you want him to be your encourager rather than your critic, say so. If you want him to stop bringing up your weight, say that too. Real conversations are not comfortable, but they are the only way forward.

**Taking Ownership of Your Health, On Your Own Terms**

Here is where nuance matters. Taking care of your health is a worthy thing to pursue. But there is a massive difference between choosing to make changes because you want to feel good and making changes because someone made you feel like you are not enough as you are.

If you decide to eat better, move more, or focus on your wellness, that decision should be yours. Rooted in how you want to feel, what kind of energy you want to have, and what kind of life you want to live. The moment that choice becomes about earning your husband's attraction back, you are building your self-care on a shaky foundation.

Because what happens if you lose the weight and he still keeps his distance? Or what happens if your body does not respond the way you hoped? Then what? Your motivation needs to be something that no one can take away from you.

If you do want to make changes, start small. Focus on consistency over intensity. More sleep. More water. More movement that you actually enjoy. Less focus on the scale and more focus on how you feel day to day. Your body will respond to care given with kindness far better than care driven by desperation.

**What His Attraction Tells You About the Marriage**

Physical attraction in a long-term marriage is real and it matters. It would be dishonest to pretend that it does not. But attraction in a healthy marriage is also not a rigid, unchanging thing. It deepens or weakens based on connection, communication, shared experiences, and how two people treat each other over the years.

If your husband's attraction to you has dropped sharply because your body changed, that is worth examining as a couple. Is your intimacy strong? Are you still emotionally connected? Are you friends? Because attraction rarely lives in isolation. It is almost always connected to the overall health of the relationship.

A husband who is emotionally close to his wife, who feels respected and seen, who is an active partner in building a life together, that husband tends to find his wife attractive even as her body changes over time. And the reverse is also true. When a marriage is disconnected and communication has broken down, small physical changes can feel much bigger than they actually are.

So this conversation is not just about your body. It is about your marriage as a whole. And that is actually a wider door to walk through than it might seem right now.

**Deciding What You Are Willing To Accept**

At some point, you have to ask yourself a hard question. What kind of marriage do I want, and is this one moving toward that or away from it?

You deserve a husband who sees you fully. Who can be honest without being cruel. Who is invested in your wellbeing, not just your appearance. If your husband is willing to grow, to communicate better, to support you rather than pressure you, then there is real work that can be done here.

But if this comment is part of a larger pattern of criticism, if he makes you feel small on a regular basis, if your self-worth has been slowly eroding year by year, then this conversation about weight is just the latest symptom of a much deeper issue. And naming that clearly, even just to yourself, is a form of strength.

You are not obligated to shrink yourself to hold a marriage together. But you are also not powerless. You have a voice, you have choices, and you have the capacity to either rebuild something stronger or decide what you truly deserve and move toward that with your head up.

No matter how broken things may feel right now, your marriage does not have to end here. The distance, the arguments, the silence, and the feeling that you’re losing the person you once loved so deeply can change. Many couples have stood exactly where you are today and found their way back to each other stronger, closer, and more connected than before.
